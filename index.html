Prompting Guide

How to get the best results from Claude

The Prompting Mindset

Claude isn't a search engine or a magic box‚Äîit's more like a very capable colleague who needs clear direction. The more context and specificity you provide, the better your results will be. Think of prompts as creative briefs: what would you give a skilled contractor to ensure they deliver exactly what you need?

Prompting Best Practices

üéØ
Be Specific
Include word counts, format requirements, tone descriptions, and structural expectations. "Write a blog post" is weak; "Write a 1,200-word blog post with 5 sections" is strong.
üìù
Provide Context
Share relevant background: who is the audience, what's the goal, where will this appear, what came before? Context shapes output quality dramatically.
üìã
Give Examples
Show Claude what good looks like. Upload examples, paste samples, or describe the style of content you're trying to match.
üîÑ
Iterate
First drafts are starting points. Ask for revisions, adjustments, and alternatives. "Make it more conversational" or "Shorten by 30%" are valid follow-ups.

Prompt Structure Framework

For complex requests, use this structure to ensure completeness:

Prompt Template
ROLE: [Who should Claude be? e.g., "You are a senior copywriter at TaylorMade"] TASK: [What do you need? Be specific about deliverable] CONTEXT: [Background information, audience, goals, constraints] FORMAT: [Structure, length, style requirements] EXAMPLES: [What good looks like, or what to avoid] OUTPUT: [Exactly what you want back‚Äîfile type, sections, etc.]

Common Prompting Patterns

Generate multiple variations efficiently:

Batch Request
Generate 10 Instagram captions for TaylorMade driver content. For each caption: - 100-150 characters - Include 1 emoji - End with a CTA - Vary the angles: technology, distance, forgiveness, tour validation, confidence Format as a numbered list I can copy directly.

Get multiple options to test:

A/B Variations
Write 5 email subject lines for our TP5 golf ball campaign, each with a different psychological angle: 1. Curiosity-based 2. Benefit-focused 3. Social proof / authority 4. Urgency / scarcity 5. Question format Keep each under 50 characters. Indicate which you'd recommend testing first and why.

Adapt content for different markets or platforms:

Localization Request
Adapt this US product launch email for the UK and Japanese markets. [Paste original email] For UK: - Adjust spelling and terminology - Modify any US-specific references - Adjust cultural tone as needed For Japan: - Provide Japanese translation - Adapt messaging for Japanese golf culture - Note any imagery or reference changes needed Flag any elements that may not translate well culturally.

What NOT to Do

Avoid these common prompting mistakes:

  • Being too vague: "Write something about golf" gives Claude nothing to work with
  • Skipping context: Claude doesn't know your campaign history, audience, or goals unless you share them
  • One-and-done thinking: Iteration almost always produces better results than accepting the first output
  • Ignoring format: Specify exactly what you want back‚Äîword count, structure, file type
  • Massive prompts: If your prompt is 2,000 words, consider breaking into a conversation

Measuring Success

Establish Your Baseline First

Before rolling out Claude, document your current state so you can measure actual improvement:

  • Time per deliverable: How long does it take to write a product description? A blog post? A social batch?
  • Content volume: How many pieces does your team produce per week/month?
  • Revision cycles: How many rounds of edits before content is approved?
  • Agency spend: What do you currently outsource and at what cost?

Track These Metrics Post-Implementation

  • Time savings: Compare time-to-completion for similar deliverables before and after Claude
  • Output increase: Measure change in content volume with same team size
  • Quality consistency: Track brand voice adherence and stakeholder approval rates
  • Cost impact: Document any reduction in agency or contractor spend
  • Speed to market: Measure time from brief to published content
  • Team adoption: Track active users and usage patterns across the team
üìä
Set Realistic Expectations

Results vary by use case and team. Some tasks (like generating social post variations) see immediate efficiency gains. Others (like strategic documents) may see quality improvements rather than time savings. Measure what matters for your specific workflows.
