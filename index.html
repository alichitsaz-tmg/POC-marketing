Key Principles for AI Success

1. Be Specific and Contextual

The single biggest factor in AI output quality is prompt specificity. Vague prompts produce generic results.

Weak Prompt
Write a social media post about our new driver.
Strong Prompt
Write a 150-word Instagram caption for the Qi35 driver launch targeting competitive amateur golfers (handicap 5-15). Emphasize the new carbon crown technology and forgiveness on off-center hits. Use TaylorMade's confident, performance-driven voice. Include a CTA to visit our bio link for pre-orders. Suggest 3-5 relevant hashtags.

2. Iterate and Refine

AI conversations are iterative. Your first output is a starting point—refine it with follow-up instructions:

  • "Make it more conversational and less formal"
  • "Shorten to 80 words and add more urgency"
  • "Add specific stats about the MOI improvement"
  • "Rewrite for a younger audience (25-35)"

3. Provide Examples When Possible

Showing AI what you want is more effective than describing it. Include examples of:

  • Previous successful campaigns or posts
  • Competitor content you admire
  • TaylorMade brand voice samples
  • Format/structure templates

4. Always Review and Fact-Check

AI can produce confident-sounding but incorrect information. Always verify:

  • Product specifications and technical claims
  • Pricing and availability
  • Tour player stats and endorsements
  • Competitive comparisons
Brand & Legal Considerations
Never publish AI-generated content without human review. Ensure all content aligns with TaylorMade brand guidelines. Do not input confidential business information (financial data, unreleased products, trade secrets) into AI tools without approval.

Best Practices for Image Generation

Writing Effective Image Prompts

  • Be specific about composition — Describe camera angle, framing, what's in focus
  • Specify lighting — "Golden hour," "dramatic side lighting," "soft diffused light"
  • Include style references — "Apple product photography style," "editorial fashion photography"
  • Mention aspect ratio — "16:9 for website banner," "1:1 for Instagram," "9:16 for Stories"
  • Describe mood/feeling — "Premium and aspirational," "energetic and bold," "calm and focused"
  • Be explicit about text — When including text, specify exact wording, size, font style, placement
Image Generation Limitations
AI-generated images should typically be used for concepts, mockups, and internal work—not as final production assets without review. Always verify text accuracy, check for visual artifacts, and ensure brand guidelines are followed. Real photography is still preferred for official product images.

Prompting Best Practices

1. Be Specific, Not Vague

Vague prompts produce generic results. Add details about:

  • Target audience (who is this for?)
  • Length/format requirements
  • Tone and voice
  • What success looks like
Vague
Write a product description for our new driver.
Specific
Write a 200-word product description for our Qi35 driver. Target audience: mid-handicap golfers (10-18 HCP) who prioritize forgiveness but still want distance. Emphasize the larger face (forgiveness) and low-spin characteristics (distance). Tone: confident and technical but accessible—not entry-level but not engineer-speak. End with a soft CTA to book a fitting.
2. Provide Examples

Showing is better than telling. Include examples of:

  • Content you like (from your brand or others)
  • The tone/style you're after
  • Formats that work well
Here's an example of a social post that performed well for us: "The new Stealth 2 isn't just an upgrade. It's a statement. 60% more carbon. Lower spin. More distance. Because good enough never won a major. #TaylorMade #Stealth2" Write 5 more posts in this same style for the Qi35 launch. Match the brevity, confidence, and structure.
3. Tell It What NOT to Do

Explicit constraints prevent common problems:

Write product copy for the new wedge line. DO NOT: - Use "game-changing" or similar clichés - Make unsubstantiated claims we can't prove - Sound like every other golf brand - Use excessive exclamation points - Write more than 150 words DO: - Lead with the specific benefit (spin control) - Mention the groove technology by name - Sound like a knowledgeable golfer, not a marketer - End with confidence, not hype
4. Break Complex Tasks into Steps

For big projects, guide the AI through stages:

Let's create a campaign concept together. We'll do this in steps: Step 1: First, I'll share the product details and objectives. You'll summarize back to confirm understanding. Step 2: Then brainstorm 5 creative concepts (just titles and one-sentence descriptions). Step 3: I'll pick 1-2 to develop further. Step 4: For the chosen concepts, develop full creative briefs. Ready? Here's the product info...
5. Ask for Multiple Options

Getting variations gives you choices and reveals different angles:

Give me 5 headline options for this email campaign. Include: - 2 that lead with the benefit - 2 that create curiosity - 1 that's bold/provocative For each headline, explain in one sentence why it might work.
6. Iterate and Refine

Your first output is rarely final. Give specific feedback:

  • Instead of: "Make it better"
  • Say: "The opening is too slow—start with the key benefit. Also, the tone is too formal for Instagram—make it more conversational."

Good follow-up prompts:

  • "Keep options 2 and 4, discard the others. Give me 3 more in the style of option 2."
  • "This is good but too long. Cut it to 100 words while keeping the core message."
  • "The first paragraph is perfect. Rewrite the rest to match that energy."
  • "Add more specific data points. Right now it's too vague."

What Doesn't Work

Common Prompting Mistakes

  • Emotional manipulation — "This is really important to my career" doesn't improve output quality
  • Fake urgency — "I need this in 5 minutes" doesn't make AI work faster or better
  • Role-playing for facts — "You are a math professor" doesn't make calculations more accurate
  • Being too brief — One-line prompts rarely get good results for complex tasks
  • Changing direction mid-conversation — Start a new chat for new topics

Troubleshooting

Common issues and how to fix them

Output Quality Issues

Output is too generic or "AI-sounding"

Causes:

  • Prompt is too vague
  • No brand voice guidance provided
  • No examples of good output

Solutions:

  • Add specific details: audience, tone, length, format
  • Include brand voice guidelines in your prompt
  • Provide 1-2 examples of content you like
  • Tell it what NOT to do: "Avoid clichés like 'game-changing'"
  • Ask for multiple variations and pick the best
Content is too long or too short

Solutions:

  • Specify exact word/character count in prompt
  • For shorter content: "Be concise. Under 100 words."
  • For longer content: "Develop this fully. Aim for 1,200 words."
  • Follow up: "Cut this to 150 words while keeping the key message"
AI keeps making things up (hallucinations)

Causes:

  • AI filling gaps in knowledge with plausible-sounding content
  • Asking for specific facts without providing them

Solutions:

  • Provide source material: "Use only the information I provide"
  • Include product specs, stats, and facts in your prompt
  • Ask it to acknowledge uncertainty: "If you're not certain about something, say so"
  • Always fact-check outputs before using
  • For research tasks, use tools with web search (Copilot, Gemini)
Responses stop mid-sentence or get cut off

Causes:

  • Output length limits reached
  • Request was too complex

Solutions:

  • Say "Continue" or "Keep going" to get the rest
  • Break request into smaller parts
  • Ask for outline first, then expand sections

Frequently Asked Questions

Quick answers to common questions

Is it safe to use AI tools with company information?

Yes, when using approved tools correctly:

  • Copilot (Chat & M365): Enterprise Data Protection is enabled when signed in with your TaylorMade account. Data is not used to train AI models.
  • Claude: Enterprise tier with security protections. Data handling is governed by our agreement with Anthropic.
  • Gemini: Use with work account for enterprise protections.

Still avoid entering: Highly sensitive financial data, trade secrets, unreleased product details, and personal employee information unless specifically needed and approved.

Will AI replace my job?

No. AI is a tool that amplifies your capabilities—it doesn't replace human judgment, creativity, and strategic thinking. Marketers who use AI effectively will:

  • Produce more work in less time
  • Spend less time on first drafts and repetitive tasks
  • Have more time for strategy, creativity, and relationship-building

AI outputs still require human review, brand alignment, fact-checking, and strategic direction. The goal is to make you more effective, not to replace you.

Can I publish AI-generated content directly?

No—always review before publishing.

AI-generated content requires human review to:

  • Verify accuracy of facts, specs, and claims
  • Ensure brand voice and guidelines alignment
  • Check for anything that sounds "off" or generic
  • Add unique insights and perspectives
  • Ensure legal/compliance standards are met

Think of AI as producing a strong first draft, not final copy.

Which tool should I use for [specific task]?

Quick guide:

  • Working in Office apps (Word, Excel, PPT, Outlook) → M365 Copilot
  • Long-form writing (blog, email sequences, strategy) → Claude
  • Image creation or editing → Nano Banana Pro (Gemini)
  • Quick research or questions → Copilot Chat
  • Unsure? → Start with what you have open. You can always try another tool.

See the Which Tool to Use section for detailed guidance.

How do I get better results from AI?

The biggest factors in output quality:

  1. Be specific — Include audience, tone, length, format in your prompt
  2. Provide context — Brand guidelines, product info, examples of good work
  3. Show examples — "Write in this style: [example]"
  4. Iterate — Refine outputs with follow-up feedback
  5. Say what NOT to do — Prevent common problems explicitly

See the Prompting Guide for detailed techniques.

Does AI learn from my conversations?

Within a session: Yes, the AI remembers what you've discussed in the current conversation and can reference it.

Between sessions: Generally no—each new chat starts fresh. Exceptions:

  • Claude Projects: Can maintain context across chats within a project
  • M365 Copilot Notebooks: Can reference added materials

Training the AI models: No—enterprise tools don't use your data to train the underlying AI models.

What are the usage limits?
  • Copilot Chat (free): Generous limits, 15 image generations/day
  • M365 Copilot: No strict limits for normal use
  • Claude: Usage limits depend on license tier; heavy use may hit rate limits
  • Gemini: Free tier has limits; enterprise has higher allowances

For typical marketing use, you're unlikely to hit limits. If you're doing high-volume work (thousands of requests), contact IT.
